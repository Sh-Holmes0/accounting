Assets

Assets in accounting are resources that a company owns and uses to generate income and future economic benefits. Examples of assets are company equipment, vehicles, accounts receivable, prepaid insurance, and office supplies.

Assets are classified based on three criteria:

  1. Current vs noncurrent: An asset can be classified based on how soon it can be converted to cash, or its economic benefit realized.

  2. Tangible vs intangible: An asset is classified based on its physical existence.

  3. Operating vs nonoperating: An asset is classified based on whether it’s used in the core operations of the business.

Liabilities

Liabilities are amounts owed to other persons or entities as a result of a past event and involve a future settlement using cash, goods, or services. Customers and vendors can be sources of liabilities for operations. Paying taxes, fees, permits, and salaries are liabilities once they become due but aren’t yet paid. Businesses use their assets to pay liabilities.

A liability can be classified as current or long-term:

  1. Current: Due in less than 12 months or within the normal operating cycle, whichever is shorter.

  2. Long-term: Expected to be settled for a period of more than 12 months; some long-term liabilities also require the application of the time value of money (computation of compounding interest), such as leases, pensions, and notes

Owner’s Equity

Owner’s equity is the residual interest or amount that assets exceed liabilities. It also represents the amount of paid-in capital and retained earnings as a result of doing business for profit.

O w n e r ’ s E q u i t y = A s s e t s − L i a b i l i t i e s Owner’s Equity = Assets - Liabilities Owner’sEquity=Assets−Liabilities

Revenues & Expenses in the Accounting Equation

Revenue is income that results from a business engaging in the activities that it is set up to do. Expenses are bills and other costs a business must pay for it to operate and earn revenue.

Financial statements

Financial statements are written records that convey the business activities and the financial performance of a company. They are used to provide information about a company’s financial position, performance, and cash flows.

The primary financial statements include the following:

  1. Income statement.
  2. Statement of owner's equity.
  3. Balance sheet.
  4. Statement of cash flows.

The Journal

Financial statements are key goals of the accounting process. In order to prepare them at the end of an accounting period, individual financial transactions must be analyzed, classified, and recorded all throughout the period. This initially takes place in a record book called the journal, where financial events called transactions are recorded as they happen, in chronological order.

When a transaction occurs, two or more accounts are affected. There is also a dollar amount associated with each of the accounts. Determining which accounts are impacted, and by how much, is the first step in making a journal entry.

The Ledger

The ledger is the second accounting record book that is a list of a company’s individual accounts list in order of account category. While the journal lists all types of transactions chronologically, the ledgers separate this same information out by account and keep a running balance of each of these accounts.

Each account has its own ledger page. The account name appears across the top.

IMPORTANT:

  1. Information entered in the ledger is always copied from what is already in the journal.
  2. An account’s running balance typically appears in either the Debit or the Credit column, not both..
  3. The process of copying from the journal to the ledger is called Posting.

Trial Balance

  • The total of all the debit balances in a company’s ledger accounts must always equal the total of all the credit balances.
  • A trial balance is a list of all a business’s accounts and its current ledger balances (copied over from the ledger accounts).
  • A trial balance may be generated at any time to test whether total debits equals total credits. It is simply a worksheet to check for accuracy before preparing financial statements.

IMPORTANT:

If both of the Total columns do not equal, there is an error that must be found and corrected.

Statement of owner's equity

  • The statement of owner's equity is prepared after the income statement.

  • It shows the beginning and ending owner's equity balances and the items affecting owner's equity during the period.

  • These items include investments, the net income or loss from the income statement, and withdrawals.

  • Because the specific revenue and expense categories that determine net income or loss appear on the income statement, the statement of owner's equity shows only the total net income or loss.

Balance Sheet

The balance sheet shows the balance, at a particular time, of each asset, each liability, and owner's equity. It proves that the accounting equation is in balance.

A s s e t s = L i a b i l i t i e s + O w n e r ′ s E q u i t y Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity Assets=Liabilities+Owner′sEquity

The ending balance on the statement of owner's equity is used to report owner's equity on the balance sheet.

Adjusting entries

  • Adjusting entries are special entries made just before financial statements are prepared at the end of the month and/or year.
  • They bring the balances of certain accounts up to date if they are not already current to properly match revenues and expenses.
  • Many ledger account balances are already correct at the end of the accounting period; however, some account balances may have changed during the period, but have not yet been updated.
  • This is what you will do by making adjusting entries, and this will ensure that your financial statement numbers are current and correct.

IMPORTANT:

  1. Each adjusting entry will always affect at least one income statement account (revenue or expense) and one balance sheet account (asset or liability)

There are two types of adjusting entries:

  • Deferrals, "Deferred" means "postponed into the future"
  • Accruals, "Accrued" means "to grow over time" or "accumulated"
  1. Deferred expenses In this case you have purchased something in “bulk” that will last you longer than one month, such as supplies, insurance, rent, or equipment
  2. Deferred revenue In this case a customer has paid you in advance for a service you will perform in the future.
  3. Accrued expenses In this case someone is already performing a service for you, but you have not paid them or recorded any journal entry yet.
  4. Accrued revenue In this case a customer will only pay you well after you complete a job that extends more than one accounting period.

Depreciation

  • A fixed asset is a tangible/physical item owned by a business that is relatively expensive and has a permanent or long life, more than one year.

  • Examples are equipment, furnishings, vehicles, buildings, and land.

  • Each of these is recorded as an asset at the time it is purchased.

  • Its initial value, and the amount in the journal entry for the purchase, is what it costs.

  • Although fixed assets cost a company money, they are not initially recorded as expenses.

    • ( Notice in the journal entry of Example (2) that the debit account is “Equipment,” NOT “Equipment Expense”).

  • Fixed assets are first recorded as assets that later are gradually “expensed off,” or claimed as a business expense, over time.

  • The process of “expensing off” the cost of a fixed asset as it is “used up” over its estimated useful life is “Depreciation”.

    • (NOTE: Land is property that does not “get used up;” therefore it is not depreciated.)

Classification Of Costs

Costs can be classified into different categories depending upon the purpose of their classification:

  1. Classification by nature or element.
  2. Functional classification.
  3. Classification on the basis of behaviour.
  4. Classification on the basis of managerial decision making and control.

Classification by nature or element.

  1. Direct Cost: These are costs directly attributable to producing a product.

  2. Indirect Costs: These are the costs which can not be assign to any particular cost unit

3. Classification on the basis of behaviour.

  1. Fixed costs: Costs which do not vary with the level of production are known as fixed costs.
  2. Variable Cost: Costs that vary in direct proportion to the volume of production.
  3. Semi-variable costs: Costs which contain both fixed and variable components are called semi -variable costs
h2 id="break-even-point">BREAK EVEN POINT
  • That point of sales volume at which total revenue is equal to total cost.
  • It is a point of no profit no loss
  • At Break Even:

    • Total Revenue (Total sales) = Total Cost

    • Total Revenue = Fixed Cost + Variable Cost

      • Where: Total Cost = Fixed Cost + Variable Cost

    • Fixed Cost = Contribution Margin

      • Where: Contribution Margin (CM) = Total Revenue - Variable Cost

    • Net Income = Contribution Margin - Fixed Cost = 0

    • Contribution Margin = Total Revenue - Variable Cost

    • Contribution Margin Ration = (Total Revenue - Variable Cost)/ Total Revenue

The Break Even Point (B.E.P) can be computed in terms of:

  1. Units of Sales Volume:

    Break Even Point in Units = Fixed Costs / UCM

     - Where:
  
         Unit contribution margin(UCM) = Revenue per Unit – Variable Cost per Unit
  
         UCM = Unit selling price – Variable Cost per Unit

  1. Budget Total or in terms of Money Value

    Break Even Point in Dollars = Fixed Costs / CM Ratio

     - Where
  
         CM ratio = Total contribution margin / Total sales
  
         CM ratio = Unit contribution margin / Unit selling price

Net Income Computation Given The Break Even Point:

  1. Break Even Point in Units

Net Income = (Sales in Units – Break Even Point in Units) * UCM

  1. Break Even Point in Dollars

Net Income =(Sales in Dollars – Break Even Point in Dollars)*CM Ratio
